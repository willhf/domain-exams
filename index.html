Domain Exams

Domain Exams offers independent examinations that assess mastery of undergraduate-level subjects. Examinations are written and proof-oriented where appropriate.

Initial offerings focus on a small number of subjects in mathematics and theoretical computer science, such as linear algebra, probability, discrete mathematics, or algorithms. Offerings are deliberately limited in scope and emphasize depth of understanding rather than breadth.

Examinations prioritize reasoning, justification, and the ability to apply ideas in unfamiliar contexts. They are defined by explicit scope and standards rather than relative performance among candidates, and are intended to distinguish genuine understanding from mechanical application of standard techniques.

The first examinations will be held in fall 2026 (dates and locations TBD).

What this is

  • A set of written examinations in clearly defined subjects.
  • Designed to measure understanding, not rote memorization.
  • Results intended for personal validation and external reference.

Why you might be interested

  • To demonstrate that your understanding meets the level expected of an honors-level undergraduate course.
  • To give your self-study a clear external objective, rather than relying on informal completion of materials.
  • To obtain a durable, independent record of having demonstrated your knowledge of a subject.

What you receive

  • A printed certificate of result for outcomes of Mastery or Proficiency.
  • A public result page under an opaque, unguessable URL, which you may share at your discretion. Result pages are not indexed for discovery, but anyone with the link may view them.

How results are reported

Results are reported using a small set of qualitative outcome categories rather than numeric scores.

  • Mastery — Demonstrates a level of understanding consistent with the intended undergraduate standard. Reasoning is correct, coherent, and robust across problems.
  • Proficiency — Demonstrates solid understanding of core material, with generally correct approaches but with some gaps, errors, or uneven depth.
  • Partial — Demonstrates familiarity with some concepts, but lacks the consistency, completeness, or rigor expected at the target level.
  • Insufficient — Does not demonstrate understanding at the target undergraduate level.

Results are not rank-ordered and do not correspond to percentages or percentiles. Many candidates should expect not to receive a result of Mastery.

Why this exists

I began studying abstract algebra to better understand modern cryptography and zero-knowledge proofs. I spent evenings working through a standard undergraduate text and its exercises, but found it difficult to stay motivated without a clear measure of achievement. I looked for a standardized subject examination that could provide an objective benchmark but could not find one. Domain Exams grew out of that experience.
