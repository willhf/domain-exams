Domain Exams

Independent examinations assessing mastery of undergraduate subject material. Written, proof-oriented when appropriate, and evaluated by human examiners.

Pending interest and feedback, initial examinations will focus on a small number of undergraduate subjects in mathematics and theoretical computer science, such as linear algebra, probability theory, discrete mathematics, or algorithms. Initial offerings will be limited in scope and designed to assess depth of understanding rather than breadth, with examinations offered only where a sufficient number of candidates are interested.

What this is

  • A set of rigorous examinations in specific domains.
  • Designed to measure understanding, not rote memorization.
  • Results intended for personal validation and external reference.

Why you might be interested

  • To demonstrate that your understanding of a subject meets the level expected of a honors-level undergraduate course.
  • To give yourself a clear, external objective for self-study, rather than relying on completion of textbooks or online materials.
  • To obtain an independent assessment of your understanding, separate from grades, transcripts, or internal course evaluations.
  • To test whether you can solve unfamiliar problems under exam conditions, not merely reproduce familiar arguments.
  • To have a durable, verifiable record of your knowledge of a subject.

What you receive

  • A printed certificate of result for outcomes of Mastery or Proficiency.
  • A public result page under an opaque, unguessable URL, which you may share at your discretion. Result pages are not indexed for discovery, but anyone with the link may view them.

How results are reported

Examinations are evaluated by human examiners. Results are reported using a small set of qualitative outcome categories rather than numeric scores.

  • Mastery — Demonstrates a level of understanding fully consistent with the intended undergraduate standard. Reasoning is correct, coherent, and robust across problems.
  • Proficiency — Demonstrates solid understanding of core material, with generally correct approaches but with notable gaps, errors, or uneven depth.
  • Partial — Demonstrates familiarity with some concepts, but lacks the consistency, completeness, or rigor expected at the target level.
  • Insufficient — Does not demonstrate understanding at the target undergraduate level.

Results are not rank-ordered and do not correspond to percentages or percentiles. Many candidates should expect not to receive a result of Mastery.

Why this exists

I began studying abstract algebra to better understand modern cryptography and zero-knowledge proofs. I spent evenings working through a standard undergraduate text and its exercises, but found it difficult to stay motivated without a clear measure of achievement. I looked for a standardized subject examination that could provide an objective benchmark but could not find one. Domain Exams grew out of that experience.
